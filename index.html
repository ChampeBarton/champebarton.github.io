Going to the Movies? Expect to See White.

Film dialogue from 2,000 screenplays, broken down by race and age

With the purple glint of energy-absorbent vibranium and the seductive thrill of a Michael B. Jordan head nod, Black Panther has thrust a moment of reflection upon audiences: When’s the last time all the characters I rooted for were black?
The question has lent the movie cultural and historical import, needling its way into just about every Black Panther thinkpiece and controversy since the movie’s release.
But in that ongoing discussion, there has been an exhausting lack of data. Just how many movies have black leads? How does that figure change with genre? With era? With box-office revenue? When black actors do get hired, do they speak as much as their white counterparts? Do I really have reason to clap a bit harder for T’Challah than for Iron Man when the credits roll?
Until we answer these questions, the statement of a movie like Black Panther will remain in some way hollow, with one foot in hypothesis and one in reality. Therein lies the purpose of this project. Following from an identical analysis of gender and film dialogue by Polygraph’s wunderkind, The Pudding, I compiled the number of words spoken by white and nonwhite characters across 2,000 films in order to put the above doubts to rest. In the process, I may have generated the largest public database of film and race, ever.
The results of that analysis were, in two words, unambiguous and dismal. We can begin examining them with a look at off-screen actors, analyzing dialogue and race for Disney animated films.

Out of 24 films (including those from Pixar), only Mulan had a script where nonwhite actors spoke more than 60% of the dialogue. Movies like Toy Story and Monster’s Inc., by contrast, clocked in at nearly 95% dialogue spoken by white actors, despite having majority non-ethnic characters (i.e. toys and monsters). In children’s movies, this lack of diverse voices can translate to a lack of exposure for audiences at their most impressionable.

But those are just animations — it’s pretty difficult to hear race…

This is true, but widening our scope to look at all 2,000 films in the database doesn’t do us much better. After running facial recognition tests on every available actor’s IMDB photo, 78% showed up white, defined here strictly to mean caucasian (i.e., fair-skinned latinos and asians count as as nonwhite). The U.S. population is only 62% white, according to the U.S. Census Bureau, meaning that whites are substantially over-represented in film.

What’s worse, just over 5,500 actors couldn’t be classified, so I assigned them “nonwhite” race values to avoid polarizing the data. (To exclude 5,000 characters may have biased the data towards the endpoints by lowering the number of characters per film, and thus the number of values that affect a script’s overall percentage). This version is the one you see above, and includes 23 million arbitrarily assigned nonwhite words. Still, the gap between the number of words spoken by white and nonwhite actors persists, and is vast.

But I see errors in there. Like Frozen — those characters aren’t all nonwhite!

As Matt Daniels and Hannah Anderson mentioned in their gendered dialogue piece, these findings aren’t definitive: a script can center around a character who doesn’t talk much (as in Mulan), scripts can change from words to screen, and in some cases I simply index the wrong actor (sorry, Elsa). But again, as Daniels and Anderson put it in their analysis, the findings are directionally accurate — over so many screenplays the trend that emerges would not be thrown off by a handful of quiet leads or database errors, and I overcompensated for such errors in favor of a subtler difference with the 23 million arbitrarily assigned words.

But for your sanity, let’s take a second and talk about...

Methodology

Daniels and Anderson over at The Pudding mapped characters with at least 100 words of dialogue in each of the 2,000 screenplays they analyzed to the person’s IMDB page. Any character who didn’t breach this 100 word threshold wasn’t counted, and so some films with 100% white or nonwhite dialogue may in reality contain 99.5% one way or the other.

Each screenplay has over 90% of its dialogue categorized by race, and I’ve had trouble finding a subset apart from Tyler Perry films that doesn’t over-index white. In addition, many of the individual films that over-index nonwhite directly concern the nonwhite experience (e.g. Boyz N The Hood, Beasts of No Nation, The Color Purple, Fruitvale Station). This, as opposed to, say, a science fiction film like Blade Runner, which deals with artificial intelligence and detective work.

How many screenplays have nonwhite characters as lead characters?

One reason for this disparity is that only 15% of the films in the dataset had nonwhite leads (i.e. nonwhite characters with the most dialogue). The heroes of our stories — the wizards or detectives or badass secret agents — are overwhelmingly white. Relegating nonwhite characters to supporting roles in film sends an unconscious message to viewers that nonwhite characters should occupy supporting positions in life, secondary to the sparkling-toothed white male lead.

Age in Hollywood: White characters vs. Nonwhite characters

Danilels and Andersons also compiled and made public age data for each of the characters in the database (i.e., the age of the actor when they played the role).

Percent of Dialogue by Actors’ Age

Among 2,000 Screenplays, all genres, all years

Unlike their findings for gender, however, whites and nonwhites had nearly identical age distributions in the industry. This 1) isolates gender as the culprit for age-based differences in character dialogue, and 2) makes clear that nonwhite actors in every age range speak fewer words than their white counterparts. In other words, the bias towards white characters is consistent regardless of age, meaning veteran nonwhite actors are just as likely to be out-spoken by white actors as are their younger counterparts.

Why I made this

The Pudding’s gendered dialogue analysis finally poured concrete over the idea that women — aside from pay issues and gross harrassment — experience a systemic form of discrimination down to the smallest unit of measurement. Following the monumental success of films like Black Panther and A Wrinkle in Time, and the important conversatoins they've started, I thought a race-based analysis was overdue.
